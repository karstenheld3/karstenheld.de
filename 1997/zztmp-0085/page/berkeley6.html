Disabled students

The Disabled Students' Program (DSP) assists students with physical disabilities, visual and hearing impairments, learning disabilities, non-visible conditions, chronic illnesses, and temporary disabilities. Services include help with enrollment and registration, emergency transportation, academic advocacy, computer consultation, support groups, attendant referral, housing, financial advising, equipment loans, interpreters, readers, notetakers, typists, laboratory assistants, and access to state-of-the-art technology. DSP has a limited number of vouchers for free Black Lightning lecture notes (230 Golden Bear Center; 642-0518, TDD 642-6376).

Bay Area Outreach and Recreation Program (BORP) provides and facilitates recreation and sports for physically disabled people. Free classes include swimming, body conditioning, wheelchair basketball, and tennis. BORP also sponsors outdoor adventure trips like skiing, rafting, and hiking. Volunteers are welcome (830 Bancroft Way, Berkeley; 849-4663).

Cal STAR offers disabled faculty, staff, students, and community members integrated sports and recreation activities in the Department of Intercollegiate Athletics and Recreational Sports. Volunteers assist members in a variety of activities such as rockclimbing, skiing, river rafting, swimming, or lifting weights (RSF; 643-8031).

The Disabled Student and Alumni Placement Program helps disabled students and alumni in all aspects of career planning and placement. Some of the services include assistance with career choice, employer contacts, interviewing skills, and developing summer, intern, part-time, and other work opportunities (Career and Graduate School Services; 2111 Bancroft Way; 642-0592, voice or TDD).

Most campus computing facilities, including The Golden Bear Center Computer Lab, have assistive equipment (84 Golden Bear Center).

The Library offers an array of special services and equipment for students with physical or learning disabilities. Just type NEWS DISABLED on any GLADIS online (computer) catalog terminal to get the name and telephone number of a Disabled Users' Library Representative for the library unit you want to use (Jo D Wright; Yig 643-0913).

Students with visual impairments or disability-related needs for adaptive equipment can use the Disabled Students' Technology Center located in Moffitt Library. The Center houses a variety of adaptive equipment, including a closed-circuit television, computers with screen-enlargement and voice-synthesis software, scanners, Braille printers, and audio cassette players (211 Moffitt Library; 642-1931).

Publications include:
"Library Services for Users with Physical and Learning Disabilities," Library Orientation Leaflet LOL#59

For eligible students, the Disabled Students' Program will subsidize the use of the library BAKER Service which delivers books and photo copies to the DSP office (642-0518, TDD 642-6376).

Students with families

Spouse and domestic partner privileges
This is a partial list of the services your husband, wife, or domestic partner can use. In some cases the services are only available to legally married couples, and most domestic partners will need domestic partner certification (available through San Francisco and Berkeley City Halls). Call ahead to find out what's required to prove the relationship to each of the units below. If you're interested in a particular department that's not listed, just call their office and ask about partner privileges. Please note that the fees listed were for the 1995-96 academic year and are likely to increase. All of these services are described elsewhere in Resource; just check the Index.

A La Carte Dining Cards: married partners pay the same rate as students for a 5% discounted prepaid food card (2401 Bowditch Street; 642-1524).

Career and Graduate School Services: married partners may use the job board for a $30 per semester fee (2111 Bancroft Way; 642-1716).

Couples counseling: one person must be a registered student. Check the Counseling section for fee information. Family counseling is available to straight, gay, bisexual, and transgender couples, sisters, brothers and other adult family groupings, and roommates (Tang Center; 2222 Bancroft Way; 642-9494).

Health services and insurance: married and domestic partners may pay fees to use the University Health Services, and students can purchase a separate health insurance policy for their partners and kids (Tang Center; 2222 Bancroft Way; 642-2000).

Housing rental listings: Married and domestic partners must bring a photocopy of the student's Cal Photo ID with a current registration sticker, something showing that the student and spouse/partner live at the same address (e.g. checks or copies of both driver's licenses), and a note from the student explaining why the spouse or partner needs to use the service (Community Living; 2405 Bowditch Street; 642-3642).

Campus housing: contact Family Student Housing (2401 Bowditch Street; 642-4109).

International student families: contact The Centre at the YWCA (2600 Bancroft Way; 642-9460).

RSF memberships: spouses and domestic partners of Berkeley students qualify for special rates; currently the fee is $99/semester. Call ahead to find out how to get a membership (Cal Rec Club; 2301 Bancroft Way; 642-7796).

Parents

Child Care Services serve approximately 190 children, ages three months to eight years in five centers near or on campus. Parents are encouraged to work in the centers two hours each week in a variety of roles. The Child Care Program is open Monday through Friday from 8am to 5pm. The Infant Program's scheduling pattern varies from this system. Students who need evening childcare are encouraged to link up with other parents to take care of each other's children.

If you are a registered student at Berkeley you are eligible to apply; fees are based on family income. Fall semester applications are due June first. Spring semester applications are due mid-November (2537 Haste Street; 642-1827).

Bananas Child Care Information and Referral Service is a very parent-friendly community organization with lots of information on childcare and other parenting issues (5232 Claremont Avenue, Oakland; 658-0381).

The Student Parent Project provides resource information, generates campus support, and advocates for responsive campus policies and services. Projects include: a 2-unit fall semester DE-Cal class; a drop-in resource room (252 Golden Bear Center) where nursing, napping, and diaper changing are welcome; assistance in setting up child care swaps and parent support groups; and a parent locator/beeper loan service to enable your child's care programs or school to reach you on campus in an emergency (Women's Resource Center; 250 Golden Bear Center; 643-9758).

Publications include:
Once Upon a University, a guide for student parents at Berkeley

Strawberry Canyon Recreation Area (SCRA) has a play area and wading pool for kids. Passes are available for Berkeley students and their children; currently the rates are $3.75/day/person or a seasonal membership (April 1 through September 26) is $85 for the first family member, $65 for the second, and then $20 each for any other family members. SCRA offers summer athletic and recreation camps and classes for children and private and group swimming lessons (lower Centennial Drive, behind Memorial Stadium; 643-6720).

Gay, lesbian, bisexual, and transgender students

As a gay, lesbian, bisexual or transgender student, college may be your first opportunity to "come out of the closet." Cal and the surrounding community have many resources to offer you.

Student groups
There are several gay, lesbian, and bisexual student organizations registered at the University including: Bi-Rap Group; BLOOD (Bound by Love Of Our Diversity); The Pink Agenda; Boalt Hall Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual Caucus; CAL B GAY (Cal Asian Lesbian/Bisexual/Gay Alliances Younited); GLOW (Gays Lesbians of Wurster); La Familia (chicana/o and latina/o); Lesbian, Gay and Bisexual MBA Student Association, Queers in Public Service (graduate students), and Lavender Grads (check Student organizations, pages 100-103).

The Multicultural Bisexual Lesbian Gay Alliance (MBLGA) organizes special social activities and weekly meetings, and distributes a Bay Area resource guide (305 Eshleman Hall; 642-6942).

Religious groups
Bisexual, lesbian, and gay Catholics can meet at Newman Hall (2700 Dwight Way; 848-7812).

Pa'amayim is for bisexual, lesbian, and gay Jews (Berkeley Hillel; 2736 Bancroft Way; 845-7793).

Academic
The Minor Program in Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender Studies offers four courses: UGIS 20, UGIS/Women's Studies 145, UGIS Women's Studies 146, and ISF/Anthropology 147B. This minor in queer studies in located in the Division of Undergraduate Interdisciplinary Studies (301 Campbell Hall; 643-6929).

Support services
The Campus Advisory Committee on Lesbian/Gay/Bisexual/Transgender Concerns consists of undergraduates, graduate students, faculty, staff, alumni, and community members appointed and reporting to the Vice Provost. It is charged with advising and making recommendations on policies and procedures to improve the quality of campus life for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender students, faculty, and staff, and informing and educating the campus community about issues that affect them (Vice Provost's Office; 642-6474).

The Queer Resource Center (QRC) empowers bisexual, lesbian, and gay students with information, advocacy, peer counseling and training, and by serving as a home base for student groups, events, and conferences that explore the queer perspective. QRC peer educators present workshops on gender and sexual orientation issues for residence halls, classes, and student groups. QRC maintains an extensive archive of printed and electronic documents and resources for the b/l/g community in the office and on the WWW and Gopher network servers. The Queer Student Orientation (Queer-SO) is in the fall (305 Eshleman Hall; 643-8429).

Coming out groups and individual and couples counseling are available through the Counseling and Psychological Services' Gay/Lesbian/Bisexual Counseling Program which is located in the University Health Service. Weekly drop-in groups are available (Tom Merrifield; Tang Center, 2222 Bancroft Way; 642-9494).

Publications include:
University Health Services: A Guide for Bisexual, Lesbian and Gay Students

The Pacific Center for Human Growth in Berkeley provides low-cost, drop-in groups and counseling and is the East Bay information hub of community resources, publications (like Lavender Pages), and social events (2712 Telegraph Avenue; switchboard number 841-6224 or business line 548-8283).

Scholarships
The UCGALA Alumni Leadership Scholarship awards one $1000 scholarship based on undergraduate academic and leadership achievement with preference to students who are gay, lesbian, bisexual or active in gay issues. Applications are available in September (Scholarship Office; room 5 Alumni House; 642-7281).

The Markowski-Leach Scholarship Fund awards 10 scholarships of up to $1000 each, every academic year to gay and lesbian-identified students who are upper-division or graduate level and who are likely to make a contribution to the gay and lesbian community at large. The fund is administered through the San Francisco State University Development Foundation. Eligible students from UC Berkeley, Stanford, and SF State can apply (Financial Aid Scholarship Office; 642-3303 or SF State University Development Foundation; 415/338-2517).

Graduate students

Graduate Division
If you want to know more about the basic rules governing graduate study at Berkeley, the Graduate Division is your ultimate resource. Final approval of the major milestones of graduate study--from admission to choice of major adviser to filing your thesis or dissertation--rests with the Graduate Division, third floor of Sproul Hall. If you're in the neighborhood, you'll also find handouts on strategies for succeeding in graduate school.

Other help to be found at the Graduate Division includes:

The Fellowships and Appointments Office, which administers the University fellowship competition, also serves as a resource center for information on extramural grants. Be sure to check their fellowship listings if you're seeking financial support, and contact your department for information concerning block grant fellowships (318 Sproul Hall; 642-0672).

The Graduate Opportunity Program provides entering underrepresented students with need-based financial assistance. The program also co-sponsors a special student orientation and sponsors a reception for these new and continuing students each fall. Upon request, the Graduate Opportunity Program also assists in locating tutoring for underrepresented minority students (316 Sproul Hall; 642-5727/643-6010).

The Graduate Student Instructor (GSI) Teaching and Resource Center advises and helps prepare GSIs in all disciplines for teaching. It offers fall and spring orientations and teaching conferences for new GSIs. The FORUM workshop series each semester focuses on issues of concern to all graduate students who teach. The center also offers Course Improvement Grants for GSIs who want to enhance their classes, sections, or labs (301 Sproul Hall; 642-4456).

Associate Deans can help you with problems that cannot be resolved in your department (424 Sproul Hall; 642-5472).

Publications include:

GSI Teaching News, newsletter (642-4456)

Graduate Assembly (GA)
The GA is Berkeley's graduate student government, composed of student representatives from the different graduate programs on campus. The GA elects three Officers and the other members of the Executive Committee (Anthony Hall; 642-2175).

There are two aspects to the GA's mission. First, the GA is charged with giving voice to graduate student concerns, both on the Berkeley campus and at the statewide level. Second, in addition to providing representation for graduate students, the GA also offers services designed to improve their experience of graduate school. These services include programs that fund graduate student projects; offer advice and counseling for women and minority students; distribute important information to the graduate student community; and assist all graduate students with especially difficult or unusual problems.

In serving graduate students the GA takes special care to provide advocacy on those issues inadequately addressed elsewhere in the campus community including student fees and financial aid, the rights of graduate student employees, student and faculty diversity, sexual and racial harassment, student housing, childcare for student parents, and student health coverage.

Confusing titles
Graduate Student Instructor (GSI) or Teaching Assistant (TA)
The two titles refer to the same thing. The Berkeley campus combined "Teaching Assistant" and "Teaching Associate" into one title, "Graduate Student Instructor." This title streamlines graduate student teaching appointments by consolidating them under one title with four salary levels. Keep this in mind when you see flyers for workshops for GSIs and TAs: they are intended for the same audience.

Graduate Student Researcher (GSR) and Research Assistant (RA)
Again, the title of Graduate Student Researcher replaced the old titles of Research Assistant, Postgraduate Researcher, and Junior/Assistant Specialist. There are six salary steps available in the GSR title.

Transfer students

With a couple of years of college already behind you, transfers have less time than other students to become familiar with the campus and surrounding community. To maximize your Berkeley experience, take an assertive stand and look into programs offered through your department or major. This guide will give you some essential tools to survive. Pay careful attention to these sections: Majors, Career and Graduate School Services, Student organizations, and Re-entry students. (Check the Index, page 112.)

Support
The Transfer Student Association (TSA) is an ASUC student-initiated service group, which serves the needs of 1500 new transfer students.

TSA does community college outreach in the Bay Area and offers a one-on-one "Day at Cal." A prospective transfer student follows an enrolled student for a day, seeing what classes are really like, meeting new people, and getting an in-depth tour of the campus. For new students there's the New Transfer Student Orientation in early fall semester where you can ask questions, meet new people, and learn about TSA. And, once new transfers have started school, TSA hosts social events like trips to San Francisco, picnics, and pizza nights, as well as academic events like graduate school advising, career planning, and résumé writing workshops. TSA also has a buddy program where a new student is paired with a continuing transfer student who shows the new student the ropes.

Since the group was established in the summer of 1990, one of TSA's biggest accomplishments has been the designation of specific residence hall buildings as transfer student residences. This way, transfer students have the option to live with other "older" students.

If TSA doesn't have the answer, they'll help you find it (316 Eshleman Hall; 642-8151).

The Transfer Student Advisory Council identifies issues specific to transfer students and underrepre-sented groups and makes recommendations to AAD and the Assistant Vice Chancellor of Educational Development. The Council assists in developing and implementing activities to enhance the transfer student's academic and social experience (Maria Lucero Padilla, Academic Achievement Division; 2515 Channing Way; 643-6286).

Housing
The Housing Office is reserving a high-rise building, Davidson Hall, in Unit II for transfer, upper division, and graduate students (642-4108).

Academic
The Academic Achievement Division (AAD) Transfer Program provides individual advising, counseling, and academic support services for transfer students including:

The "Transfer Student Perspective Course," Ethnic Studies 98/198 (2 units, P/NP) helps new transfer students enhance their academic skills at Berkeley. The class provides general advising, course planning, personal counseling, and introduction to specific campus support services. Success, skill development, writing assessment and diagnosis, critical reading, and study strategies are emphasized.

Transfer Student Tutoring provides upper division writing tutorial support across disciplines to transfer students. Students can receive individual diagnostic assessment and evaluation; one to one and group tutoring; assistance with papers and personal statements for graduate school; seminars and workshops on specific writing topics and related concerns; and computer-assisted writing and research support.

If you want to help tutor other transfer students, you can take an Education 197 seminar for credit (1-4 units, P/NP).

The Computer Instruction Program offers Macintosh workshops and courses for credit. Learn: word processing; e-mail; library searches; advanced search techniques using Internet; planning, organizing, and formatting research papers (Tom O'Brien, 643-9569).

The Academic Achievement Division is located a few blocks south of campus at 2515 Channing Way (Maria Lucero Padilla, 643-6286).

Re-entry students

Support
The Re-entry Program provides academic and non-academic support services to undergraduates 25 and over and to graduate students 30 and over. The program offers monthly and weekly events to encourage students to work and study collaboratively. Services include no-fee, drop-in writing tutoring and peer advocacy; special workshops like "Preparing for Graduate School;" student/faculty dinners; coffee socials; and an annual achievement and graduation ceremony (260 Golden Bear Center; randeeg@uclink4.berkeley. edu; 643-8070).

The Re-entry Students Club (RSC) is an ASUC group which plans social and community service activities for re-entry students.

The Re-entry Network is a group of staff representing 25 departments who provide advocacy and resource information for re-entry students.

A monthly orientation explains UC's undergraduate and graduate admissions process to prospective students.

The Re-entry Achievement and Graduation Ceremony celebrates re-entry students' achievements and contributions to the University.

Housing
The Convent provides housing for 20 re-entry and graduate students. Vacated by nuns, the facility has large communal areas, and all rooms are singles. Students of color

Many students feel isolated when they go to college, and this experience can be intensified if you find yourself the only person of color in a classroom, department, or residential unit. Fortunately, a variety of support services and student organizations for both graduates and undergraduates can help.

Some services are outlined in other sections of Resource. For instance, the Professional Development Program (PDP), an honors program, helps women and underrepresented students succeed in academic and professional fields. The Academic Achievement Division offers academic support services and personal and financial aid assistance. Project DARE's interactive programs help students, staff, and faculty work effectively in a multicultural environment. The Center for Racial Education encourages cross-cultural awareness and supports two publications which voice concerns of women and men of color. Be sure to look in the Index for more information.

You may be interested in working with your community as an intern or volunteer; check the Volunteer and Internships sections for more details. There are also over 50 student of color organizations listed in the Extra matter under Student organizations.

Academic
Ethnic Studies provides multi-disciplinary and comparative study of African American, American Indian, Asian American, and Chicano experiences and communities (506 Barrows Hall; 643-0796).

Career and Graduate School Services seeks to increase the number of students of color entering graduate or professional schools. An adviser specializes in servicing traditionally underrepresented students. The Name Exchange circulates the names and educational plans of Berkeley minority students to other campuses (2111 Bancroft Way; 642-5207).

Publications include:
Continue the Journey...An invitation to underrepresented minorities and women to explore graduate and professional studies

Summer Bridge Program offers entering freshmen an intensive six week program of classes, academic workshops, and counseling seminars to ease the transition from high school to the University. Counseling, advising, and structured activities are continued into the first year through Bridge Extension (New Student Programs; 137 Golden Bear Center; 1/800/273-8110 or 642-5983).

Housing
International House offers a US Citizens, Permanent Residents Room and Board Scholarship for Residence to encourage ethnic and economic diversity at I-House. Applicants must be of junior, senior, or graduate student status, and have a demonstrated financial need. Students of color are encouraged to apply; the award covers room and board for an academic year and for spring semester (Jacqueline Boggan, Personnel Office; I-House; 2299 Piedmont Avenue; 642-0517).

Support
California Alumni Association (CAA) Mentorship Programs match Berkeley alumni with students who have similar career, major, or extra-curricular interests. Participants meet formally as a group several times a semester. CAA's hope is that alumni will share insights into career opportunities and the experiences which contributed to their own successful matriculation at Berkeley. Six programs currently exist: African American, Asian/Pacific, Chicano/Latino, Multicultural, Pilipino American, and Gay/Lesbian/Bisexual (Alumni House; 642-4976).

Women of Color Agenda is a student initiated project which provides a place of belonging and empowerment. The Agenda facilitates classes, forums, and special projects. Internships are available (Women's Resource Center; 250 Golden Bear Center; 642-4786).

Student Life Advising Services (SLAS) offers multi-cultural counseling and advising to Educational Opportunity Program students (low-income, first generation, California resident, college students) and underrepre-sented ethnic minority groups: African American, Chicano, Latino, and Native American. The unit provides students with alternatives when confronted with decisions, concerns, or problems that may affect their progress toward graduation. Particular emphasis is placed on helping students develop problem-solving skills (SLAS; 140 Golden Bear Center; 642-7224). See page 47 for more information.

African Americans

Support
The Office of African American Student Development coordinates academic development, activities, and services to address the specific needs of African American students on campus by providing networking and referral and individual and group counseling. The African American Student Agenda of activities, including Black Student Day, Black Arts Forum, The Black List, Black History Month and Kwanzaa, is coordinated through this office as well (Grace Massey; 293 Golden Bear Center; 642-0096 or 643-0442).

Black Welcome Week, the first of a series of programs and activities to help orient African American students, is an annual student-initiated event for students, staff, and faculty in early fall semester. Receptions and activities introduce new students to African American support services, faculty, staff, and students (642-0096 or 643-0442).

Black Recruitment and Retention Center (BRRC), an ASUC student-run program, helps African American students with problems they may face before or after they come to Berkeley. BRRC makes recruitment trips to more than 100 high schools throughout Northern and Southern California in order to encourage students to continue their education. Study halls, social events, and other forms of academic assistance especially tailored to the needs of African American students are offered by BRRC to help increase the retention of Black students at Cal (311 Eshleman Hall; 642-6224).

National Pan Hellenic Council (NPHC) is the umbrella organization for predominantly African American sororities and fraternities (642-7950; check pages 71 and 103).

Academic
The Department of African American Studies offers a Bachelor's degree and a minor program in the study of the African Diaspora, and a graduate program will be available soon. African American Studies offers 1A and 1B courses which fulfill the L&S Reading and Composition Requirement as well as courses that satisfy humanities and social science breadth and American Cultures requirements (660 Barrows Hall; 642-7084).

The African American Studies Reading Room houses periodicals, videos, books, and audio tapes, as well as reserve readings for courses and a special reference collection (675A&B Barrows Hall; 642-0351).

The UNCF/Merck Science Research Scholarship, jointly sponsored by the United Negro College Fund and Merck&Co, Inc encourages African American undergraduate students to further their science education and pursue science careers. The award provides funds for educational expenses, two paid summer internships at a Merck facility, and research funds for the recipient's major department. African American juniors in the life and physical sciences are encouraged to apply. Applications are due in early February for the next academic year (UGIS; 301 Campbell Hall; 643-6929).

The Immigrant Student Program (ISP) coordinates campus services and programs for immigrant students. Support services include the Fall Orientation to introduce new immigrant students to campus, Ethnic Studies 98 "Introduction to the University, An Immigrant Perspective" (2 units, P/NP), internships (1-2 units), cultural events, career seminars, and a newsletter, WhISPers. For more information, contact the ISP coordinators (Student Activities and Services; 102 Sproul Hall; 643-8457).

Pilipino American Student Orientation (PASO) is an annual program sponsored by Pilipino Academic Student Services (643-9302), Pilipino American Alliance (642-6766), Pilipino American Society for Architects and Engineers, and Pilipino Association for Health Careers (643-9302). All new Pilipino students will receive invitations in late summer for the orientation which is held during the first week of fall semester.

Chicanos/ Latinos

Support
The Chicano/Latino Office of Student Life Educational Development is an advocate for all Chicano/Latino students and their issues on the Berkeley campus and provides administrative support to all Chicano/Latino student groups and their activities, including Chicano/Latino Parent Orientation, Chicano/Latino Student Reception, an orientation class, Cinco de Mayo, La Voz and the Chicano/Latino Leadership Conference (Lupe Gallegos-Diaz; 291 Golden Bear Center; 642-1802).

Raza Recruitment and Retention Center aims to increase the enrollment of Chicano/Latino high school students into institutions of higher education. Volunteers visit area high schools to encourage and motivate students to pursue academic skills.

Once at Berkeley, the Center supports new students through the Camarada Program (a buddy program), study halls, rap sessions, and social hours. Students can also find out about earning academic credit through community or campus volunteering (500 Eshleman Hall; 642-1322).

Academic
The Chicano Studies Department offers an interdisciplinary curriculum which focuses on the historical and contemporary experiences of people of Mexican descent in American society. Two courses, Chicano Studies 1A and 1B, fulfill the L&S Reading and Composition Requirement (510 Barrows Hall; 8am-noon, 642-0240; 1pm-5pm, 643-0796).

If you're feeling overwhelmed by any kind of problem, Maria Alvarado, adviser in the Chicano Studies Department, can help you identify available campus resources.

The Chicano Studies Library is located in 104 Wheeler Hall (642-3859).

Latin American Studies, an undergraduate group major in the College of Letters and Science presents a balanced curriculum of the history, society and culture of Latin America for students wishing a broader perspective of the area than is usually available through a departmental major. Latin American Studies also offers interdisciplinary MA and PhD degrees (International and Area Studies Teaching Programs; 207 Moses Hall; iasugtp@garnet. berkeley.edu; 642-4466).

The Center for Latin American Studies facilitates the work of more than 100 faculty with Latin American expertise and promotes research and training on Latin America across the University. The Center also sponsors seminars and events pertaining to the region (2334 Bowditch Street, between Durant and Channing; 642-2088).

Housing
Casa Joaquin Murieta is the only independently owned and operated Chicana/o cooperative in North America. Located in a fully equipped mansion one block from campus, Casa houses 40 students and offers academic, financial and personal counseling, on-site computer facilities, retreats, tutoring, internships, and scholarships. Casa also serves as an off-campus center for orientations, lectures, readings, classes, seminars, cultural events, recruitment activities, and fundraisers.

For more information, contact The Frente Foundation (2336 Piedmont Avenue, Berkeley, CA 94704; 540-1984).

Casa Magdalena Mora is a cross-cultural theme program for 32 Spanish-speaking residents who share interests in Chicano/Mexican relations and culture. For housing applications and more information, write to Residential and Family Living (2401 Bowditch Street, Berkeley, CA 94720-2272; 642-3213).
