Academic advising

Undergraduates
Whether you have an academic adviser or not depends on what school or college and department you've been admitted to and your status (first year or transfer). Almost everyone who is not in the College of Letters and Science (L&S) is assigned an adviser prior to their first semester. This person helps with academic planning and is available for general consultation.

L&S undeclared undergraduates, including new frosh and transfer students, in the College of Letters and Science are not assigned a specific adviser. However, appointments are available with a diverse group of L&S advisers who provide academic counseling and help in program planning for undergraduates. For quick questions, you can receive prompt responses by e-mail: kpasaLnS@violet.

The College advisers are the primary advisers for undeclared students. They also conduct advising workshops for new students, give special assistance to students on academic probation, and offer specialized workshops for students (e.g. "Major Attraction: A Workshop on Choosing Your Major"). College advisers can help new students with credit evaluations of courses taken at other institutions and units assigned to Advanced Placement scores. The College advisers act as the representatives of the Dean of the College in approving student petitions. For more information about advisers or academic planning, you should read your College Announcement, which is mailed to you along with a welcome packet, or is available from the college or school.

Graduate students
Most new graduate students are assigned a graduate adviser in their department when they first arrive on campus. This faculty member helps students decide on which courses they need to take. A major adviser, who will direct a graduate student's research and thesis or dissertation, is usually chosen by the student, rather than assigned, and usually after the first year of graduate school.

Undergraduate majors
Every student must select a field of concentration and pursue a major or curriculum, normally by taking a minimum number of units in one school or department. Occasionally, as in Business Administration or Optometry, the school and the department are the same. Admission to these schools is at the upper division level (after completion of 60 units of lower division requirements for Business Administration or 90 for Optometry). In Chemistry, Engineering, Environmental Design, and Natural Resources, you are admitted to the major you indicated on your application.

In Letters and Science (L&S), however, because of enrollment limitations, you must seek admission to a major program after entering the University. You may declare your major when you have completed 30 units and all pre-major requirements, and you must declare by the time you have completed 60 units. Junior transfer students must declare a major by the beginning of their second term (113 Campbell Hall; 642-1483).

Although students may have indicated a desired major on the application for admission to the University, this does not guarantee admission to the major; students must still file a petition with Letters and Science. To declare a major in Letters and Science, obtain a petition from the College office or department and consult the department which sponsors the major for information on acceptance to the program. If you are accepted and the form has been completed and signed, return it to the department or College office. Be sure to read the descriptions of the various majors in the General Catalog. Faculty, department advisers, and fellow students in the major of interest can provide valuable insight. The College advisers in L&S provide guidance on choosing a major with individual appointments and workshops.

All new first year students will be mailed a copy of Smart Start: Guide to your first two years in the College of Letters and Science which contains information about L&S majors.

Changing your major
Changing your major is as easy as declaring a major and happens just about as often. If you decide that the major you have chosen is not right for you (and often it's hard to know this until you have attempted some upper division course work in the subject), you need to see an adviser immediately to review what other majors you may be prepared for. Once you're a senior it may be very difficult to complete a new major within the unit maximums allowed by your school or college. If you cannot complete a new major within an appropriate time frame, the dean of your school or college may deny your petition to change your major. For this reason, it's critical that you plan a flexible program at the lower division level, in consultation with advisers, to give yourself options if you change your mind about your major.

Individual majors
If you have sound academic reasons for developing your own major, a minimum GPA of 3.0, and you develop a program in consultation with a faculty member who will serve as your adviser, you may submit an Individual Major application. Students are required to include a senior thesis (Undergraduate and Interdisciplinary Studies; 301 Campbell Hall; 642-0108).

The individual major may not duplicate other existing majors on campus. And, students should investigate the Interdisciplinary Studies Field major prior to attempting to declare an individual major.

Double majors
Students can declare a double major as long as they are able to complete requirements for both disciplines within 140 units, and they file lots of forms. It's even possible to pursue simultaneous degrees which would be two majors in two different colleges (e.g. L&S and Natural Resources). Deans of both colleges must approve the application (Office of Undergraduate Advising in L&S; 113 Campbell Hall; 642-1483).

Minors
While each student must complete a major, graduation with a minor is optional. The purpose of minors is to foster coherence in the academic work students complete outside their majors, and to provide official recognition by the University for such work.

There are currently 47 minors in the College of Letters and Science, 10 minors in the College of Environmental Design, and a minor in the School of Business Administration. Information on the various minors may be obtained from the school or college and from participating departments.

Planning for the future

Counseling and Psychological Services (CPS)
If you're unsure of what career you want to go into, start with a visit to CPS. They offer information on career counseling and testing (Tang Center; third floor, 2222 Bancroft Way; 642-9494).

The Counseling and Psychological Services Career and Educational Guidance Library has an extensive collection of materials for exploring educational and career options, including college catalogs, directories of graduate schools, and career books. There are interactive computer systems to help students discover occupations that match their unique skills, values, and interests (the small building in front of the Tang Center; 2220 Bancroft Way; 642-2367).

Career and Graduate School Services (CGSS)
CGSS can help you with career planning and the graduate school admission process while you're still in school and after you graduate. By meeting with advisers and attending programs and workshops, you'll learn how to define your career objectives based on factual information about the world of work and an understanding of what you have to offer, given your education, life experience, and interests. CGSS teaches job search skills which will assist you in finding a job consistent with your needs, provides extensive job and internship listings and on-campus interviewing for employment opportunities.

CGSS also offers advice and assistance with application to medical, law, and other graduate schools, as well as informational handouts. The Letter of Evaluation Service, where you can store recommendation letters for future use, will send letters to graduate schools upon your request.

In the CGSS Resource Center you will find occupational and graduate school information along with a calendar of workshops and programs (2111 Bancroft Way; 642-5207).

Professional Development Program
The Professional Development Program (PDP) is an honors program designed to encourage minority and women students to realize their full academic potential for careers in academic and professional fields. Undergraduates are offered academic counseling, workshops, and placement in faculty-supervised research. PDP also provides graduate students with special orientations, workshops, and guest lectures. Additionally, students are assisted with obtaining internships and post-doctoral placement (230B Stephens Hall; pdpdata@garnet.berkeley.-edu; 642-5881).

Student services

Student Life Advising Services (SLAS)
SLAS provides students with academic, personal, financial, and career counseling and advising services. Professionally trained staff help students develop problem solving and self-management skills. The goal of SLAS is to motivate and direct students toward achieving academic excellence and to increase the number of low-income first generation and under-represented students that graduate from Berkeley. Counselors and peer advisers are available on both a drop-in and appointment basis to assist students with their concerns.

In addition to counseling and advising services, SLAS administers a wide range of programs to assist students in achieving their educational and career goals (140 Golden Bear Center; 642-7224).

The Achievement Awards Program honors a select number of EOP/AA students each year with awards for academic achievement and community service.

The Reduced Study-Load Program is designed for students admitted to the University by Special Action and/or those students mandated to enroll in this program. Participants are also required to enroll in an academic skills development option.

The Peer Advising Program enhances students' social, personal, and academic adjustment to Berkeley.

The Schedule Request Review requires a SLAS counselor to approve mandated students' course selections for an academic year.

University YWCA
In addition to volunteer placements, the University YWCA has many services and programs for students.

English in Action matches international students and scholars with native English speakers to learn the language and about American culture.

Turning Point Career Center offers a library of resources and job listings, support groups, classes and workshops on self-assessment, résumé writing, and interviewing.

Youth Mentor Program matches UC students with at-risk high school students. Matches meet weekly with Cal students serving as role models; applications for mentors are available in the fall.

Health, fitness, and personal enrichment are available in over 30 classes and workshops offered every semester including aerobics, international and ballroom dancing, and self defense, communication skills and self-esteem enrichment.

Student Council designs and implements a wide variety of programs of interest to students including: career nights, rape awareness, drug abuse awareness, and multicultural programs.

The Women's Resource Library offers a vast array of books by and about women (2600 Bancroft Way at Bowditch, Berkeley; 848-6370).

Religious holidays and exam conflicts
Faculty are encouraged to take religious holidays into account when planning exams and assignment due dates. If you are aware of a potential conflict, discuss it with your professor as soon as possible.

Beware
Some cults use religion as a hook to pull in unsuspecting people; be sure to read the Cult awareness section (page 20) and to check with URC if you are unsure about a religious group.

Counseling

Counseling and Psychological Services (CPS)
CPS, a part of the University Health Services, provides career, academic, and personal counseling through individual sessions, couples counseling, group sessions, testing services, and occupational information. Counselors are a multicultural group of social workers, psychologists, and psychiatrists.

Any Berkeley student can come in to talk about: deciding on a major, learning about interests and abilities, clarifying career goals, coping with personal crisis, dealing with concerns about family or other relationships, feelings of anxiety or loneliness, stress, gay and lesbian issues, what it's like to be at Berkeley, the effects of racism, or any of the many issues we all encounter. Talking with a counselor in a confidential, non-judgmental atmosphere can aid self-understanding and solution of personal issues.

The first two appointments are free; after two, there is a $12 charge per appointment. For groups there is a one-time fee of $20 for each group (Tang Center; 2222 Bancroft Way, third floor; 642-9494).

CPS offers workshops, training and consultation for student groups regarding various psychological issues.

Social Services staff provide counseling and advocacy for sexual assault and alcohol/drug use; information and referral; and support for those with medically related issues (642-6074).

Peer advising

Student to Student Peer Counseling and Referrals (SSPC) is an ASUC peer counseling group designed to meet the needs of Berkeley students. The counselors, a diverse, non-judgmental and unbiased group of students, are available on a drop-in or phone-in basis. Services include counseling on a wide variety of issues and an extensive referral service for more specialized help. All services are free and confidential. New counselors are selected during the first week of classes each semester, and academic credit is available (300C and 318 Eshleman Hall; 642-9021).

Procedural intervention

ASUC Student Advocate
The Student Advocate is elected every year during ASUC elections. With the help of several assistants, the Student Advocate helps students with University-related problems, concerning admissions, dismissals and transfers; financial aid problems; sexual harassment; student conduct; grade disputes; procedural problems; and UC housing or employment (204Eshleman Hall; 642-6912).

Office of the Ombudsperson for Students
The Ombudsperson assists students with problems that seem insolvable by the usual procedures and agencies. If you have reached a procedural impasse or believe you have been treated unfairly in a University-related matter, the Ombudsperson may assist you in effecting a satisfactory solution (642-5754).

Note
The Student Advocate and the Ombudsperson both help students with University grievances. The Ombuds-person, unlike the Student Advocate, plays a non-advocacy role in problem resolution.

The Office of Student Conduct (OSC)
OSC investigates and resolves cases of individual and student organizational misconduct, including allegations of academic dishonesty, forgery, hazing, inappropriate use of University resources, computer-related crimes, misuse of alcohol or drugs, physical abuse and threats of violence, and racial or sexual harassment or assault.

The Code of Student Conduct is enforced on all University owned or operated properties, at all University sponsored events, and within a limited geographical area surrounding the campus.

OSC works in conjunction with academic departments, residential units, health services, and campus and local police to ensure that the campus environment is as safe and productive as possible. If you would like a copy of the Code or have any questions, drop by or call (326 Sproul Hall; 643-9069).

The Observer Program
This program was developed during the sixties by concerned faculty members who felt that neutral campus staff should observe campus events where there was a possibility of a conflict. Berkeley staff volunteers act as impartial witnesses at volatile campus events, and they provide objective reports and a calming effect (Student Activities and Services; 102 Sproul Hall; 642-6772).

Sexual harassment

The Title IX Officer addresses concerns, questions, and complaints regarding sexual harassment. She provides information and referrals about sexual harassment and investigates complaints. Contact Carmen McKines for more information (200 California Hall; 643-7985).

The Women's Resource Center provides information and support in responding to and resolving sexual harassment experiences involving students. A staff resource specialist provides counseling, and can discuss options and explain campus policies and procedures (250 Golden Bear Center; 643-5730).

SHAPE Project (Sexual Harassment Advocacy and Peer Education) interns offer workshops to student groups and residential living units. Academic credit is available (250 Golden Bear Center; 643-9861).

The University Health Service coordinates education programs, crisis counseling, advocacy, and medical care for women and men who have been harassed or assaulted (Tang Center, 2222 Bancroft Way: Prevention, 642-7202; Victim Assistance and Counseling, Social Services, 642-6074 or Counseling and Psychological Services, 642-9494; or Medical Care, 642-3188).

Legal services

Student Activities and Services has an attorney on staff who can advise you regarding your legal rights and obligations if you have a problem or question about the law. The attorney is available Mondays, Tuesdays, and Wednesdays, 9am to 4pm, during the fall and spring semesters and Tuesdays, 9am to 4pm during the summer. You need your current Cal Photo ID for an appointment for free consultation. If you require legal representation, you will be referred to appropriate resources (102 Sproul Hall; 642-4980).

The ASUC Student Legal Clinic (SLC) is a comprehensive legal resource center which enables students to make informed decisions about legal problems. The Clinic offers information regarding legal problems on topics such as: bankruptcy, consumer issues, contracts, court appearances, divorce, immigration, insurance, small claims, and traffic violations. Services include attorney and agency referrals and Income Tax assistance. The SLC does not give legal advice (300A Eshleman Hall; 642-9986).

Jury duty
You may be called to serve as a juror if you are 18 years of age or older and have been a resident of your county for 30 days before being selected. Student status does not exempt you from serving, although the court will sometimes postpone your appearance in cases of midterms or finals conflicts.
