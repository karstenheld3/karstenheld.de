Special programs


We wanted to call your attention to some learning and teaching opportunities that you may not find in the General Catalog.

Student taught and initiated courses
The two programs listed offer students a way to create and teach their own class. A third way is to find a faculty sponsor for a Field Study (97/197/297), Independent Study (99/199/299) or Directed Group Study (98/198/298) class. (The 90s are lower division, 190s are upper division, and the 290s are graduate.) Check your College or School Announcement for more information.

Project for Collaborative Learning
Do you want to teach or develop your own class? The Project for Collaborative Learning is a resource for students or groups interested in collaborative learning and student-run courses. You can take a one-unit class to learn about collaborative learning, develop a proposal, and then apply for a stipend to actually complete your project.

Three existing courses (see Student Learning Center, page 42), Ethnic Perspectives, Other Voices, and Unseen America form the initial core of the program (Student Learning Center; Golden Bear Center; 643-5737).

DE-Cal (Democratic Education at Cal)
DE-Cal is an ASUC-sponsored organization which offers 25 to 30 student-initiated, student-taught classes for credit each semester. Some past courses have included:

Most classes are small, offer a high degree of student participation, and the subject matter is usually outside the traditional curriculum. You can add DE-Cal classes during the Tele-BEARS adjustment periods. Course listings are not available in the General Catalog or the Schedule of Classes, but you can find out about them at the ASUC Bookstore, at the CalSO Resource Center, and through the DE-Cal office (320 Eshleman Hall; 642-9127).

DE-Cal is a good alternative for students who need to add classes. Since DE-Cal classes won't accept enrollment until the first day of instruction, you'll have just as good a chance as anyone else to get in. And, DE-Cal classes are sure to add some zing to a traditional course load.

Freshman Seminar Program
The Freshman Seminar Program arose from the conviction that early intellectual contact with faculty members would greatly enhance the freshman experience at Cal. Beginning in the fall semester 1992, professors from every campus department have joined to offer an impressive array of new seminars. The courses numbered 24 (and in some cases 90) bear one unit of credit; they are limited to fifteen students, and freshmen will be given priority for enrollment.

The other new seminars, most of which are numbered 39, are limited to twenty-five (or fewer) freshmen and sophomores.

Seminars, which emphasize interaction and discussion, provide a nice counterpoint to the learning experience in our campus' large lecture halls. These seminars also offer lower-division students an unprecedented opportunity to explore a wide range of majors and even fields of study usually reserved for graduate students. A brochure describing each semester's seminar offerings is mailed to each freshman in time for Tele-BEARS registration (301 Campbell Hall; 642-8378).

Tutor for credit
You can serve your community and earn units for doing so by tutoring in local schools. You earn one unit for every three hours per week in a classroom or tutoring center under the courses Education 97 (lower division students) and Education 197 (upper division). You can tutor almost any subject, at any grade level, at almost any time of day, in any number of schools. Check the bulletin board on the first floor of Tolman Hall, outside by the Student Services Office (School of Education; 642-5345).

Summer Sessions
Berkeley Summer Sessions offers over 350 courses in 65 academic departments and is an ideal way to accelerate your academic program, learn a language, or make up a deficiency. Over the course of twelve weeks you can complete a full semester of coursework; there are five different sessions to choose from, varying in length from three to ten weeks and beginning as early as May or as late as July. All courses carry regular academic credit and will be recorded on your UC Berkeley transcript.

Summer Sessions Catalogs are mailed to all Berkeley undergraduates in March. To add your off-campus friends to the mailing list, contact Berkeley Summer Sessions (22 Wheeler Hall; summer@uclink. berkeley.edu; 642-5611).

Research

One of the best ways to get involved in Berkeley's vast research opportunities is to ask the department secretary from a major department if he or she knows of a research project which fits your interests, or ask your professors. There are over 40 Organized Research Units directed by Berkeley professors and staff which can be found in the front Administration section of the Campus Telephone Directory or in the back of the General Catalog. Research opportunities for students are also listed in the CGSS publication, The Internship Directory (2111 Bancroft Way; 642-1716).

McNair Post-baccalaureate Achievement Program
This program prepares Berkeley undergraduates for graduate study leading to a PhD. Fifty juniors are selected by competition to participate in academic-year, credit-bearing courses, summer research internships with Berkeley faculty, advising, and academic strengthening activities. Low-income, first-generation college students and members of groups underrepresented in graduate studies are encouraged to apply (Academic Achievement Division; 2515 Channing Way; 642-7935).

Summer Research Opportunities for Minority Undergraduates
Underrepresented minority students (Alaskan Native, American Indian, African American, Mexican American, Puerto Rican or Filipino, and Latinos, Asian Americans, and women in a field of study where they are underrepresented) can apply to participate in an eight-week, full-time, faculty-sponsored, stipend-financed, research internship in the humanities or physical and social sciences for the summer. Continuing sophomores, juniors, and non-graduating seniors with a 3.0 GPA and an interest in pursuing graduate school are encouraged to submit an application, due February 1.

Other opportunities for undergraduate minority and women students are available. Check the publication, Continue the journey, or visit the Graduate Opportunity Program (Graduate Division; 316 Sproul Hall; 643-6010).

Undergraduate Research Apprentice Program
Highly motivated undergraduates can apply for semester or year-long opportunities to work closely with senior faculty on research projects. Undergraduates with a 2.0 GPA or above are eligible to apply and those selected may enroll in Undergraduate and Interdisciplinary Studies (UGIS) 192 (1-4 units, P/NP). Descriptions of projects are available in November and April for the following semesters (341 Campbell Hall, 642-3795).

University Research Expeditions Program (UREP)
Join a UC Research Expedition, explore uncharted territories, preserve threatened environments, and unravel ancient mysteries while working side-by-side with a UC scientist on field research projects worldwide. UREP provides funds and student scholarships for field research in the natural and social sciences while simultaneously offering students, staff, and the general public educational field opportunities. Student scholarship applications for summer projects are due mid-March (2223 Fulton Street, fourth floor, desk CO3; urep@uclink. berkeley.edu, 642-6586).

Multicultural opportunities

Our diverse campus environment offers many unique ways to learn more about yourself, others, and the world in which we live.

Student Diversity Awareness Programs
Project DARE (Diversity Awareness through Resource and Education) and ME and U (Multicultural Exchange and Understanding) are experiential-based educational programs that orient students to multicultural issues in the context of promoting student retention and enhancing the campus climate for underrepresented groups (Academic Achievement Division; Maria Lucero Padilla; 2515 Channing Way; 643-6286).

The Center for Racial Education
The Center for Racial Education works to increase the level of cross-cultural awareness among members of our diverse campus community. The Center conducts educational workshops, DE-Cal courses, and special events which familiarize students, faculty, and staff with each other's cultural backgrounds and concerns. The Center also offers services that assist individuals, living groups, and work places to overcome inter-racial tensions and conflicts (312 Eshleman Hall; 643-9921).

I-House (International House)
I-House offers a large number of cultural, educational, and recreational programs which encourage international discussion and understanding. I-House is a place where you can learn first-hand about the cultures and politics of other countries without leaving the US. All new students can become members of the I-House at a special discount rate. As a member, you can participate in many activities for free, and the Program News, a monthly publication announcing upcoming events, will be mailed to your home. Programs include aerobics, language tables, social dance, cultural performances, political discussions, trips and tours throughout the Bay Area, a Speakers Bureau for local schools, and arranging host families for international students (2299 Piedmont Avenue; 642-9460).

Education Abroad Program (EAP)
EAP allows undergraduates and graduates to experience higher education at 100 host universities and colleges in 33 countries. Offerings include full-year study programs as well as short-term and special-focus programs. Interested students should visit the EAP office in the fall prior to the year they wish to go abroad; there is a minimum GPA requirement of 3.0. The Study Abroad Office library has information on other study abroad programs (160 Stephens Hall, 642-1356).

UC Extension programs abroad
The Summer Oxford/Berkeley Program offers three-week summer study programs at Worcester College, University of Oxford. Course selections include history, literature, political science, economics, and anthropology.

The Paris Program offers a three-week, three-unit, summer study program in France. Courses are taught at a central Paris location by professors from all over France. Subjects include art history and history, and some courses are taught entirely in French.

You can get information about these and other noncredit travel/study programs at 415/252-5229.

Note
Acceptance of Extension credit is not automatic. You should consult with the dean of your college or school before enrolling in an Extension course for degree credit.

Peace Corps
Berkeley is the number one contributor of total number of Peace Corps volunteers. Since 1961, more than 3000 Cal students have been placed in assignments in over 100 countries all over the world. Assignments are for two years with transportation, medical support, and monthly allowances provided. Recruiting is year-round, and most programs require a degree. Contact the Peace Corps recruitment office to find out how you may qualify (415/744-2677 or 1-800/424-8580).

International student IDs
Council Travel can issue International Student IDs which will make you eligible for discounts abroad. You will need $18, a photo ID, an extra passport photo, and University enrollment verification such as a Cal Photo ID or a transcript (2486 Channing Way; 848-8604).

Passports
The US Main Post Office in Berkeley accepts applications for US Passports. You'll need $65, a birth certificate and a California state driver's license or state ID, and two passport photos (2000 Allston Way, room 109; 649-3178).

International and Area Studies
Berkeley's international centers for research and outreach are part of our rich multicultural environment. Through concerts, lectures, special events, conferences, and seminars, you'll have opportunities to satisfy your personal and academic curiosity about other lands and cultures in over a dozen areas. Get yourself on the mailing list for events such as an Indonesian puppet show, a discussion seminar on planning for a new world order in the international economy, or a lecture on medicinal plants in Latin America. Whether you're interested in the humanities, sciences, or social sciences, you'll find the international dimension enriching to your Berkeley experience.

UC elsewhere

Center for Media and Independent Learning (CMIL)
CMIL is a statewide department of University of California Extension which offers over 150 undergraduate and professional-level courses by mail, e-mail, and fax. The program is self-paced and enrollment is always open, giving you the opportunity to complete courses at times and places convenient to your schedule (2000 Center Street, room 444; 642-4124).

One potential snag: undergraduate credit earned through CMIL is generally accepted at UC and other institutions, but check with your school or college to make sure they will accept the credits.

Education at Home Program (EHP)
EHP is a one-quarter program conducted through UC Riverside that enables undergraduates to spend eight weeks on the East Coast (Williamsburg, Philadelphia, and Washington, DC) studying primarily Early American History (Department of History, UC Riverside; Riverside, CA 92421; 714/787-3820, or contact Victor Rotenberg; 3309 Dwinelle Hall; 642-0356).

Howard University Exchange Program
A one-semester undergraduate exchange program with Howard University in Washington DC has been established for declared majors in the College of Letters and Science. Students with a minimum GPA of 2.5 are eligible to apply. For applications and information contact the Office for African American Student Development (293 Golden Bear Center; 642-0096).

Intercampus Exchange Program (IEP)
IEP allows registered graduate students to study at any UC campus. This program gives you the opportunity to associate with scholars and fields of study not available at Berkeley and to use special facilities and collections of other campuses (Graduate Degrees and Petitions; 302 Sproul Hall; 642-7330).

University Extension
UC Berkeley Extension is Berkeley's continuing education program offering additional educational opportunities at night and during the weekends for the community and Berkeley students. Extension courses cover most fields of study at levels from lower division to professional: many are interdisciplinary, focusing on new research and current developments (1995 University Avenue at Milvia; 642-4111).

One potential snag: acceptance of Extension credit is not automatic. You should consult with the dean of your college or school before enrolling in an Extension course for degree credit.

Internships

Internships complement the University experience in valuable ways. You can apply knowledge acquired in the classroom to work situations, reality-test career options, develop skills, and gain experience unavailable through coursework alone. Internships can be found through the following programs and departments.

Campus programs
Career and Graduate School Services (CGSS) publishes The Internship Directory, a very good listing of campus resources for students (CGSS Resource Center; 2111 Bancroft Way, second floor; 642-1716).

Cooperative Education Internship Program provides general internship advising and paid part-time or full-time positions in all disciplines. A co-op internship, which may be of varying duration, provides several unique benefits. In addition to career advising and a mentor-apprentice relationship with a professional, a transcript notation is also available (2111 Bancroft Way, third floor; 642-1532).

Engineering Co-op offers undergraduate and graduate students opportunities for full-time, six month long internships in engineering, computer science, or chemical engineering positions with the government and in industry. Participants are on sanctioned "leave of absence" status from the University. Faculty usually visit students on the job to monitor and evaluate the training sites (306 McLaughlin; 642-6385).

Government programs
Cal-in-Berkeley Internship Program is a joint ASUC/UC/City of Berkeley program where students can get hands-on experience working in municipal government agencies ranging from public administration to energy conservation. Internships require an 8-12 hour per week commitment, and upper division students can receive academic credit (City and Regional Planning 197, 1-3 units, P/NP). Applications are available the first week of each semester (510 Eshleman Hall; 642-9041).

Cal-in-Sacramento provides 30 students with legislative office and state agency internships during the summer. Internships are available to undergraduates and graduates and relate to every academic major and area of interest. Applications are available in early September and are due in October for the following summer. Financial aid is available (Alumni House; 642-7280).

Cal-in-the-Capital sends about 70 students to Washington DC internships each summer. Placements range from Capitol Hill to the Smithsonian museums and include many fields such as engineering, the arts, science, health, the humanities, political, economic, and law-related areas. Applications are available in early September and are due in early October. Financial aid is available (Alumni House; 642-2920).

Health and Medical Apprenticeship Program (HMAP) is a joint program sponsored by the ASUC and the School of Public Health that supplements pre-health education by exposing undergraduates to critical health care issues. The IDS 130, "Social, Ethical, and Political Issues in Health and Medicine" seminar (2 units, P/NP) invites speakers with diverse backgrounds from around the country to speak about medical ethics. The Field Study internship (2 units, P/NP) places upper division students with practicing physicians in the community. The Health Service Internship Program (2 units, P/NP) coordinates volunteer work for students interested in public health and medicine. HMAP also produces a newsletter, Issues, which presents information about health and medicine for students and the community (309 Eshleman Hall; 643-6100).

Cal Corps Public Service Center
Cal Corps Public Service Center is the campus base and informational clearinghouse for community service efforts.

The Volunteer Clearinghouse is a computerized database listing volunteer placements at over 200 community-based organizations in the Bay Area such as Habitat for Humanity, Berkeley Unified School District, San Francisco District Attorney's Office, and Project Open Hand. In addition, a new section details full-time volunteer opportunities, including the Peace Corps, City Year, and Public Allies, which students can undertake during a year off or after graduation. For students or groups who are unable to volunteer on a weekly basis, Cal Corps compiles a monthly listing of short-term service projects.

The Student Community Service Grants Program offers grants, advising, and leadership development for students initiating and operating their own community service projects. Approximately 25 groups, ranging from tutorial and mentorship programs to health and legal services, are funded each year.

Service-Learning Classes integrate community service into the academic curriculum. Students may receive 2-3 units (P/NP) for volunteering at a local agency and attending a seminar related to their service placements. Issues addressed include homelessness, tutoring, mentoring, and community development.

AmeriCorps expands Cal student community service through President Clinton's National Service Program. National Service mobilizes young Americans to address pressing educational, environmental, public safety, and human needs while enhancing the educational experience, practical skills and sense of civic responsibility of those participating. At Cal, 12 AmeriCorps members work as paid community service coordinators part-time during the academic year, full-time during the summer, and will receive an educational monetary award at the end of their service term. The AmeriCorps recruitment and application process takes place in the spring semester.

Information about all these programs is available at the Cal Corps office (505 Eshleman Hall; calcorps@ofc. berkeley.edu; 642-3916).

Cal Camp
A tradition that dates back to the 1930s, this all-student volunteer ASUC organization provides leadership and encourages children to build a positive self-image at a nonprofit camp for socially and economically disadvantaged inner-city children between the ages of 7-11. If you would like to be a camp counselor for one week during the summer, please contact Cal Camp or stop by the office (300B Eshleman; 643-5005).

Stiles Hall
Students have the opportunity to participate in several outstanding community service projects at Stiles Hall. Student volunteers are trained to: serve as mentors and role models to low income and minority youth; work with children from drug or alcohol dependent families; tutor children in structured after-school programs; form meaningful friendships with local senior citizens; serve as companions to patients at Napa State Mental Hospital; and perform own-recognizance investigations for local courts and jails. Students develop personally and professionally while addressing significant community problems. Each project requires a two-semester, 4 to 6 hours/week consistent commitment. Credit is available for most positions. Selection takes place in early fall, so drop by Stiles Hall and speak with the staff about the variety of placements (2400 Bancroft Way at Dana, Berkeley, 94704; 841-6010).

University YWCA
For over 100 years the YWCA has been training and placing students in more than 50 East Bay community agencies. Volunteer opportunities include working with Children's Hospital, Berkeley School Volunteers, Planned Parenthood, Asian Health Services, Legal Assistance for Seniors, and many more.

In addition to the Volunteer Placement Program students may also volunteer through the YWCA's Youth Mentor Program and the English in Action Program. The Youth Mentor Program matches Cal students with at-risk teens in one-to-one relationships. Mentors and their younger students make a commitment for the entire academic year. English in Action matches foreign students and visiting scholars (at UC Berkeley) with English speaking partners for weekly conversation.

Academic credit is available for many placements (2600 Bancroft Way, Berkeley; 848-6370).
